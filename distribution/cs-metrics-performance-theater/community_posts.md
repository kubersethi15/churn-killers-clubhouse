## Reddit (r/CustomerSuccess)

**Title:** Are we measuring customer success wrong? High engagement might predict churn, not retention

I've been tracking customer success metrics for years, and I'm starting to think we've got it backwards. Everyone focuses on engagement scores, login frequency, feature adoption. But I've noticed something weird: some of our highest-churning customers were also our most "engaged" in the months leading up to churn.

These customers were logging in constantly, clicking everything, reaching out to support daily. We thought they were power users. Turns out they were desperately trying to make our product work before giving up.

Meanwhile, customers who barely touched the platform but achieved their core business goals renewed at higher rates and expanded their contracts.

It makes me wonder if high engagement is sometimes a distress signal, not a health indicator. Like when someone keeps adjusting the thermostat because the temperature isn't right.

Has anyone else noticed this pattern? What metrics actually correlate with renewal in your experience?

I dove deeper into this topic and why most CS metrics might be vanity theater in a recent piece: https://churnisdead.com/newsletter/cs-metrics-performance-theater
