According to legal-ops and procurement benchmarking, a large share of enterprise software contracts contain some form of price-adjustment language. A consumer price index peg. A fixed annual uplift. An auto-escalator tied to renewal. By most published estimates, that is roughly half of enterprise deals.

Enforcement rates run a fraction of that.

Sit with the gap between those two numbers for a moment. Half your enterprise revenue base may carry a clause that lets you raise price without a sales cycle. Only a minority of those clauses actually get triggered.

The rest sit dormant. Signed by the customer. Approved by their procurement team. Written by your own legal department after a negotiation everyone has since forgotten. And never collected, because at renewal the account manager was busy pitching a new product instead of reading the contract they already had.

I have a name for this evaporating money. I call it the forgotten uplift. The compounding revenue you are contractually owed and have never collected.

## You Are Earning The Same Money Twice

Here is the motion most enterprise account teams run right now.

Retention slips. Leadership wants it back. The reflex is expansion. New products, new seats, new use cases, a cross-sell into an adjacent department. So the team builds the expansion deck, maps the buying committee, and books meetings to socialize the pitch.

All of which requires the one thing that is currently frozen. New budget.

Enterprise buyers are sitting on their wallets. In an environment where every software dollar gets an artificial-intelligence interrogation before it clears procurement, the upsell playbook everyone reaches for is precisely the motion stalling hardest.

So you are grinding a sales cycle for revenue you have to earn, against a buyer who has been told to freeze, while a price increase the same buyer already signed sits unenforced in a document nobody has opened since the ink dried.

You are earning money twice. Once when your legal team negotiated the escalator into the contract. And again when your account manager tries to manufacture equivalent revenue through an expansion motion that needs approval the escalator never required.

The forgotten uplift is the money you only had to earn once.

## Why The Clause Goes Dark

Three things conspire to make the escalator invisible.

First, low inflation trained everyone to ignore it. For most of the last decade, a price-index clause moved the number by one or two percent. Not worth the awkward renewal conversation, so teams let it slide. That habit calcified. Then inflation woke up, the clause became meaningful, and the muscle to enforce it had already atrophied.

Second, enforcement never felt strategic. There is no expansion deck for collecting an uplift. No champion-building narrative. No victory lap to post about. Compared to launching a shiny new product line, quietly triggering a contractual price increase feels like paperwork. So it loses every attention battle.

Third, nobody owns the notice date. Most escalators require a formal notice within a specific window before renewal. Miss that window and you forfeit the increase for the entire next term. The deadline lapses on a calendar no one is watching.

Every notice window that passes is money you gave away for a full year. No amount of heroic selling next quarter buys back the term you forfeited by not opening the document.

## The Five-Step Audit

Here is how to go get the money you already own.

### 1. Pull every enterprise contract

Search the language. Price-index peg, fixed uplift, auto-escalator, renewal increase. Flag every contract that contains one. You are building a list of every deal that carries a dormant price lever.

### 2. Identify the trigger

Each escalator has a mechanism and a threshold. Some peg to an inflation index. Some apply a flat annual percentage. Some activate automatically, others require you to elect the increase. Document what activates each one and by how much.

### 3. Locate the notice date

This is the deadline to enforce. If it is approaching, that is your first and easiest capture. If you have already missed it for the current term, record it in a forfeited column and immediately calendar the next window so you never miss it again.

### 4. Build the portfolio number

Produce two figures. Recoverable uplift you can still capture this cycle. And forfeited uplift that dormancy has already cost you. Bring both to your next leadership meeting before anyone sets an expansion target. The forfeited number alone will change the conversation.

### 5. Stand up the notification play

Put every notice date on a calendar the renewals team owns. Make enforcement the default and holding the documented exception. If you choose not to enforce a clause, that should be a deliberate decision with a reason, not a deadline that quietly lapsed.

## The Expansion Deck Can Wait

The expansion motion can wait a week. It is stalled on a budget freeze anyway.

The escalator cannot. Every notice window that lapses is uplift you forfeit for an entire term.

Go find the money you already own. Then go earn the rest.
