# Title: The Price Increase Your Buyer Already Signed (And Your CS Team Forgot)
# Copy everything below the line into LinkedIn Newsletter editor

---

Roughly half of enterprise SaaS contracts contain some form of price-adjustment language. A CPI peg. A fixed annual uplift. An auto-escalator tied to renewal.

Enforcement runs a fraction of that.

Sit with the gap between those two numbers for a second. Half your enterprise book may carry a clause that lets you raise price without a sales cycle. A minority of those clauses actually get pulled.

The rest sit dormant. Signed by the customer. Approved by their procurement. Written by your own legal team after a negotiation everyone has since forgotten. And never collected, because at renewal time the CSM was busy pitching a module instead of reading the contract they already had.

I want to give you the name for that evaporating money. The forgotten uplift. The compounding revenue you are contractually owed and have never collected.

Here is the motion most enterprise CS teams are running right now.

NRR slipped. Leadership wants it back. The reflex is expansion. New modules, new seats, new use cases, a cross-sell into the adjacent business unit. So the team builds the expansion deck, maps the buying committee, and books QBRs to socialise the pitch.

All of which requires the one thing that is currently frozen. Net-new budget.

CIOs are sitting on their wallets under AI scrutiny. Every incremental dollar of software spend is getting the can-an-agent-do-this interrogation before it clears procurement. The upsell playbook everyone reaches for is precisely the motion stalling hardest this quarter.

So you are grinding a sales cycle for revenue you have to earn, against a buyer who has been told to freeze, while a price increase the same buyer already signed sits unenforced in a PDF nobody has opened since the ink dried.

You are earning money twice. Once when your legal team negotiated the escalator into the contract. And again, now, when your CSM tries to manufacture equivalent revenue through an expansion motion that needs approval the escalator never required.

The forgotten uplift is the money you only had to earn once.

So why does the clause go dark? Three things conspire.

First, low inflation trained everyone to ignore it. For most of the last decade, a CPI-pegged clause moved the number by one or two percent. Not worth the awkward renewal conversation, so teams let it slide. That habit calcified. Then inflation woke up, the clause got real, and the muscle to enforce it had atrophied.

Second, enforcement never felt strategic. There is no expansion deck for collecting an uplift. No champion-building arc. No LinkedIn victory lap. So the escalator loses every attention battle to the shinier new pitch.

Third, nobody owns the notice date. The window to trigger the increase lapses on a calendar no one is watching. And every notice window that lapses is uplift you forfeit for an entire term. No amount of heroic selling next quarter buys back the year you gave away by not opening the PDF.

This is the cheapest NRR in your portfolio. Nobody posts about it because it is not heroic. It is just money the buyer already agreed to pay, quietly evaporating because enforcing a clause feels less strategic than selling something new.

The expansion deck can wait a week. It is stalled on a budget freeze anyway. The escalator cannot.

Go find the money you already own. Then go earn the rest.
