## Reddit (r/CustomerSuccess)

**Title:** Half of enterprise contracts have a price escalator clause. Why does almost nobody enforce it?

I have been digging into a gap that does not get talked about much in CS circles. Legal-ops benchmarking suggests roughly half of enterprise SaaS contracts contain some price-adjustment language: a CPI peg, a fixed annual uplift, an auto-escalator tied to renewal. Enforcement rates run way lower.

The thing is, this is revenue the buyer already agreed to pay. Signed by them. Approved by their procurement. Written by your own legal team. And then never collected because at renewal the CSM is busy pitching a module instead of reading the contract that already exists.

What makes it worse right now is the budget environment. Buyers are freezing new spend and interrogating every dollar. So teams grind expansion cycles for net-new revenue that is stalling hard, while a price increase the same buyer already signed sits unenforced.

Three reasons I think the clause goes dark: low inflation trained everyone to ignore it for a decade, enforcing a clause feels less heroic than selling something new, and nobody owns the notice date so the window quietly lapses. Miss the window and you forfeit the uplift for the whole term.

Has anyone here actually audited their escalator clauses and gone to collect? Curious whether the recoverable number is meaningful or a rounding error. I ended up writing a full audit on this for my newsletter if useful, but mostly want to hear if others have run this play.
