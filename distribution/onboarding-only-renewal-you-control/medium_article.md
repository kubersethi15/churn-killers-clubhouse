Roughly 90 days. That is how long it takes a new customer to decide, quietly and mostly without realising it, whether they will still be paying you in two years.

Not at the contract renewal. Not in the quarterly business review where you walk them through the value story. Not even in month nine when a dashboard turns yellow and someone schedules a save call. All of that is theatre performed on top of a decision that was already made much earlier, in a window most companies treat as a cost to minimise rather than the moment that matters most.

If you run a customer-facing team, that idea should change how you think about your entire job.

## The lever nobody else can touch

Almost every tool you reach for to keep a customer after they go live is a shared tool. You do not fully own it.

Pricing belongs to the revenue leader. Expansion has drifted to the sales team or a renewals desk. The product roadmap belongs to engineering, and they will not build the feature your customer wants this quarter, or next. The customer's internal politics belong to the customer, and they will not tell you about them until it is too late to do anything.

There is exactly one phase of the customer lifecycle where you hold unilateral control over the outcome. Onboarding.

You decide the scope. You decide what the first real result looks like. You decide whether one team uses the product or six. Nobody has to sign off. Nobody can veto you.

And here is what most teams do with that power. They optimise it for speed. They rush the account to go-live and hand it off as fast as the implementation plan allows.

That is the most expensive mistake in customer success. And it stays invisible because the damage does not surface for another eleven months.

## The renewal is not decided at the renewal

Here is the reframe.

The renewal is decided during onboarding. And onboarding is the last moment you get to decide it alone.

Everything after go-live is a negotiation with people who do not report to you. But the depth of the deployment, the number of teams that come to depend on the product, and whether a genuine business outcome gets agreed to by the person who controls the budget, those are things you can determine without asking anyone's permission.

We throw that advantage away because we measure onboarding with the wrong number.

## The metrics that lie to you

Time to first value. Time to go-live. Time to activation.

Every one of these measures the same thing from the vendor's point of view. They mark the day the account moves from implementing to live in your internal system and stops consuming your team's hours.

Notice what none of them measure. Whether the customer built something they cannot easily walk away from.

So let me name the number that actually matters.

## Depth of root

Depth of root asks one question. Did the product get embedded into a workflow the customer cannot rip out, or did you just give them a login?

A login is fast. It photographs well. It hits the go-live target. It also churns quietly.

A root is slow. It takes more teams, more workflow integration, and a real outcome co-signed by a budget holder. It survives.

Every day you shave off implementation to hit a speed target, you are borrowing against a renewal you will lose in month eleven. That is when a new leader arrives with their own preferred tools. That is when a budget review lands and someone asks what this line item actually does. That is when the one person who understood why they bought you takes another job.

A login cannot survive any of those three events. A root survives all of them.

## What this looks like in practice

If you want to know whether you are building logins or roots, ask these questions about your recent onboardings.

First, did success get defined as go-live, or as a business outcome that the budget holder personally agreed to?

Second, how many teams inside the customer actually use the product? One team is a login. Multiple dependent teams is a root.

Third, if your champion left tomorrow, would the account survive? If your only relationship is with one person, you have a login with a name attached.

Fourth, how hard would it be for the customer to remove you? If ripping you out is a quick task rather than a genuine project, you never rooted.

Fifth, and most tellingly, did you measure speed or did you measure depth?

## The accounts that actually hurt

Spend enough time in this work and you learn something uncomfortable.

The accounts you lose are rarely the ones that blew up. The dramatic failures, the product breakdowns, the pricing fights. You see those coming. You can explain them.

The ones that hurt are the quiet ones. The accounts that were fine. That went live early. That someone on the team was proud of and moved on from because they looked finished.

They did not leave because you failed at the renewal. They left because you won the wrong game in the first 90 days. You delivered a login when you could have grown a root, and you moved on to the next go-live before the account had anything holding it in place.

The renewal was never yours to win in month eleven. It was yours in month one. And you handed it back.

Your next quarter of new accounts is sitting right now in the only phase of the lifecycle you actually control.

What are you optimising them for?
