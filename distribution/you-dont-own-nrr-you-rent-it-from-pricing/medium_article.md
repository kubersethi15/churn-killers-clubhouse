## The number nobody warns you about

There is a moment in every SaaS company's annual planning that plays out the same way. A leader walks into a room. They are handed a net revenue retention target. And everyone in the room treats that number as a measure of how good the customer team is.

It is not.

According to Benchmarkit's 2026 SaaS Performance Metrics report, usage-based SaaS companies post median net revenue retention of around 108%. Seat-based companies come in around 95%. That is a thirteen-point gap between two businesses that might sell to the same buyers, in the same economy, with equally talented teams.

The only difference is how the contract is shaped.

No amount of relationship management closes that gap. The spread exists before a single customer conversation happens. It is baked into the pricing model.

Which leads to an uncomfortable conclusion. Most of your retention is decided by people you may never meet, in a pricing meeting you were never invited to, using a model you have no authority to change.

You do not own retention. You rent it. And the landlord is pricing.

## The assumption that used to be true

For years the logic was clean. The customer team owns retention because the customer team owns the relationship. Keep customers happy, drive adoption, catch risk early, and the numbers follow. If retention is weak, the playbook must be weak.

That assumption held for a reason. Seats used to be a decent proxy for value. More users meant more of the organization depending on the product. More dependence meant more expansion. The way you charged and the value you delivered pointed in the same direction. Nobody needed to separate them.

That era is ending.

## How AI broke the link between seats and value

Agentic AI severed the connection between the number of people using a product and the value that product delivers.

When a customer deploys AI agents that shrink the team using your software, the seat count drops. But satisfaction can stay high. In many cases the value the customer receives goes up even as the seats go down. They are getting more done with fewer people.

Here is the problem. If your contract was built to charge for headcount, headcount is exactly what your customer is now automating away.

The clearest evidence lives in customer service, where the link between seats and work snapped first.

Klarna said publicly in 2024 that its AI assistant was doing the work of roughly 700 full-time agents. Salesforce moved its own support organization from around 9,000 people toward roughly 5,000 as its Agentforce product absorbed frontline volume, and its CEO has spoken openly about not backfilling those roles. Intercom rebuilt its entire pricing around its Fin resolution bot because charging per support seat stopped reflecting the value being delivered.

Those are service seats. But the same physics is moving up the stack into the knowledge-worker tools that enterprise software companies sell every day.

The seat was always a billing convenience standing in for value. AI just exposed the stand-in.

## The framework: decompose the number

So what do you actually do with this? You stop treating the target as one number and start splitting it into two.

Here is the decomposition.

### Step one: separate inherited from influenceable

Take your portfolio's retention number and break it into two parts. The part fixed by pricing architecture, which you inherited. And the part your team can genuinely move through execution.

### Step two: run it on ten accounts

Do not try to boil the ocean. Take ten accounts and run the decomposition. Estimate how much of the target is winnable through better execution and how much was set the moment the contract was signed. The result usually surprises people.

### Step three: name the gap out loud

If most of your target lives in the pricing layer, that is not a customer success problem. That is a packaging problem wearing a customer success costume. Naming it is the whole point.

### Step four: build the Outcome Ledger

Start tracking the value each account receives that has nothing to do with seat count. Time saved. Work automated. Business outcomes achieved. This is your evidence that value and seats have diverged.

### Step five: walk it into the pricing conversation

Use the gap and the ledger to earn a voice in how the product is packaged. This is how you stop scraping the floor and start raising the ceiling.

## Renting versus buying

Think about two different customer leaders heading into next year.

The first spends the year running better adoption plays on a seat-based book. They optimize the small slice they influence while the larger slice they inherited quietly erodes underneath them.

The second spends the year building the outcome evidence and walking it into the pricing meeting. They change the architecture itself.

One is renting. The other is buying a stake in the building.

The question worth asking in every planning session is simple, and it will make the room uncomfortable. Of the number you just agreed to, how much can your team actually move, and who else in this company is accountable for the rest?

If nobody can answer, you did not get a target. You got a rent bill.
