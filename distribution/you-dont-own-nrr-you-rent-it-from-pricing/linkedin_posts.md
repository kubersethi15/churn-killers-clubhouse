Thirteen points.

That is the gap Benchmarkit's 2026 report puts between the NRR of usage-based SaaS companies and seat-based ones. Same discipline. Same buyers. Same economy.

No CSM has ever closed that gap with a better QBR.

Here is the uncomfortable truth. Most of your NRR was decided by people you have never met. In a pricing meeting you were never invited to. Using a model you have no authority to change.

You don't own NRR. You rent it. And the landlord is Pricing.

The accepted wisdom says CS owns NRR because CS owns the relationship. That logic held when seats were a decent proxy for value. More users meant more entrenchment meant more expansion.

Agentic AI just severed that link.

When a customer deploys AI agents that collapse the team using your product, seat count drops while satisfaction stays high. Your contract was built to capture headcount. Headcount is exactly what your customer is now automating away.

Stop optimizing the four points you influence while the ninety-one you inherited erode underneath you.

POST 2 -- WEDNESDAY
Strategy: Pure value with real-world proof points. Ends with a question that forces CS leaders to confront a scenario they are already seeing.
============================================================

AI just exposed the stand-in.

Look at customer service, where the seat-to-work link snapped first.

Klarna said its AI assistant was doing the work of roughly 700 full-time agents. Salesforce ran its support org from around 9,000 people toward roughly 5,000 as Agentforce absorbed tier-one and tier-two volume. Their CEO has spoken openly about not backfilling those roles. Intercom rebuilt its entire pricing around its Fin resolution bot because per-seat support licensing stopped mapping to value.

Those are service seats. But the same physics is now moving up the stack into the knowledge-worker tools that enterprise CS sells.

Here is what this means for your book. The value your customer receives can rise as seats fall. Higher satisfaction. Lower revenue. Your best relationships can retain perfectly and still contract.

And if you are measured on NRR, you take the hit for a design decision you never made.

So I'll ask the question I keep asking every VP of CS heading into planning.

Of the number you just agreed to, how much can your team actually move, and who in this company is accountable for the rest?

What happens in your org when a happy customer shrinks their seat count?

============================================================

Five steps to decompose the number you were handed.

1. Split your portfolio's NRR into two parts. The part fixed by pricing architecture. And the part your team can actually influence.

2. Run the decomposition on ten accounts. Not your whole book. Ten. Find out how much of your target is unwinnable by execution alone.

3. Name the gap. If most of your target lives in the pricing layer, that is not a CS problem. That is a packaging problem wearing a CS costume.

4. Build the Outcome Ledger. Track the value each account receives that has nothing to do with seat count. This is your evidence.

5. Walk it into the pricing meeting. Use the gap to earn a voice in packaging. Stop scraping the floor. Start raising the ceiling.

The CSM who spends 2026 running better adoption plays on a seat-based book is optimizing four points while ninety-one erode.

The CSM who builds the Outcome Ledger is changing the architecture itself.

One is renting. The other is buying a stake in the building.

Early in my CS career, I took the number personally.

Soft NRR meant soft playbook. That was the story I told myself. If the retention was weak, I hadn't run enough plays, built enough relationships, caught enough risk early.

So I worked harder. Better QBRs. Deeper adoption plans. More proactive outreach.

The number barely moved.

It took me too long to see it. Most of that target was decided long before I opened my laptop. In a pricing meeting I was never invited to. The contract was built to capture headcount, and I was being graded on it as if I had drawn the blueprint.

I was renting a number and being billed for the mortgage.

What changed everything was refusing to accept the target as a measure of my team. Not to dodge accountability. To locate it correctly. To walk into planning and ask who owns the part CS cannot move.

That conversation is uncomfortable. It is also the only one that raises the ceiling instead of scraping the floor.
