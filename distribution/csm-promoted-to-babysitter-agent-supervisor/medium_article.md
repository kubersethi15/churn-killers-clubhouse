In June, SaaS investor Jason Lemkin published a widely shared write-up declaring that the traditional customer success manager role is dead.

His evidence was a system called QB. An AI "VP of Customer Success" managing more than 150 accounts end to end, running on a team of three humans and twenty-one software agents. The detail everyone repeated: QB autonomously emailed 83 sponsors at 12:20am, each message fully customised to the specific account. No human reviewed them. No human was needed.

He is right about the work. He is wrong about the conclusion. And the gap between those two things is the most important career question in customer success right now.

When a system like QB lands inside a real enterprise, the role does not die. It splits. It splits into two jobs that almost nobody was hired to do, and most teams are about to drift into the worse half while leadership calls it a strategic transformation.

## What the AI actually does well

Start with what is true. An agent managing 150 sponsorship accounts is genuinely impressive, and I am not going to wave it away to protect a comfortable thesis.

But look at the shape of those relationships. Sponsorships are largely single-vendor and transactional. They renew once a year. The buyer already understands the value because they can count the leads they got. The work is timely, personalised, high-volume outreach.

That is exactly the kind of work software does better than a tired human at midnight. No ego. No fatigue. No forgotten follow-up.

Now move that same agent onto a different kind of account.

## Where the AI hits a wall

Picture a Fortune 500 customer. The renewal has become a board-level budget fight. The internal champion just left for a competitor. The security team flagged a data-residency problem that froze a planned expansion. And three executives are quietly fighting over who owns the platform internally.

The agent can email every one of them at 12:20am. Beautifully written. Perfectly timed. Account-specific.

None of those emails move the renewal one inch.

That account does not need more outreach. It needs judgment. Someone who can read the politics, hold a line in a procurement negotiation, and know that the right move this week might be to say nothing at all.

## The split: supervisor versus closer

The administrative core of the customer success job, the part that consumed 60 to 70 percent of a typical week, is now machine work. What remains divides into two distinct human roles.

**The supervisor.** This person manages the agents. They review what the software sends. They catch the tone-deaf message before it reaches a customer who just laid off half their team. They manage by exception across hundreds of accounts. It is necessary work. It is also far closer to quality control than to strategy, and it carries less leverage than it appears to from the outside.

**The closer.** This person owns the accounts the agents cannot touch. The board fights. The frozen expansions. The political knife fights. The closer sits in the room where the renewal is actually decided. This is the part of the job that was always the real job, even when we did not have time for it.

Here is the uncomfortable part. Most people hired into customer success during the 2021 hiring boom were hired for neither role. They were hired to "build relationships" and "be proactive." That is precisely the layer the agent just absorbed.

If your job could be done by software emailing 83 people at midnight, it was already a fragile job. The AI did not kill it. The AI made it visible.

## The old job, honestly described

Let me describe how the role actually ran for the last four years.

You carried 40 accounts. Monday morning was an inbox of escalations, a sales handoff with no notes, two fire drills in Slack, and one account threatening to leave that quarter. You wrote check-in emails between meetings. You built quarterly review decks at 9pm. You updated the CRM on Friday so the forecast looked clean.

You called this proactive work. You were proud of it. The dashboard showed your outreach cadence in reassuring green.

But be honest about what it was. It was motion. Volume. Activity that looked like value. And almost none of it was judgment.

Motion is exactly what an agent absorbs first.

## The number that decides your seat

There is one metric that tells you which job you are walking into. Call it your judgment ratio. It is the share of your week spent on work that requires reading a situation a machine cannot read.

Low ratio means you are heading for the supervisor seat. You will spend your days reviewing what the agents produce. Useful, but capped.

High ratio means you are heading for the closer seat. You will spend your days in the rooms where the hard outcomes get decided. That is where the leverage lives.

Most teams will not measure this. They will let the new org chart sort people by default, and they will quietly file their expensive relationship hires into the babysitter role while telling everyone the company is transforming.

So run the audit yourself. Track one week. Separate the motion from the judgment. Get your number.

Then decide, deliberately, which job you are building. Because if you do not decide, the org chart will decide for you.

What is your judgment ratio?
