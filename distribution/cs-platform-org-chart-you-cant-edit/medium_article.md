In 2024, two software companies merged into one. Two years later, they still ship as two separate products under one logo. Two data models. Two onboarding paths. Two completely different ways of calculating the same core metric.

If your team runs on a platform like this, here's a question worth sitting with before your next renewal.

Do you actually know which version of the product your daily workflow lives in?

If your answer is "does it matter," that shrug is the whole problem.

This isn't about any one vendor. Most enterprise software grows through acquisition, bolting on products until the underlying data model carries the geology of every deal the company ever closed. Others grow from a single founding idea about how work should happen.

None of these tools are neutral. None of them ship empty.

Every one of them arrives with an opinion about how your team should operate. And the day you adopted it, that opinion quietly started becoming your operating model.

You think you bought a system of record. You bought a system of constraint.

## The Question Nobody Asks at Renewal

Walk through a typical renewal cycle for a six-figure software contract.

Someone pulls usage stats. Someone confirms adoption looks healthy. Finance asks if there's a cheaper tier. Procurement negotiates a discount. A leader signs.

Nobody asks the only question that matters.

How much of how we work exists because we chose it, versus because the tool made it the easiest path?

That question never gets asked, because the people who could ask it have stopped seeing the tool. It has become furniture. It's the room they work in, not an object in the room.

You don't audit the floor you're standing on.

So you re-sign. And in re-signing, you re-buy a thousand small decisions you never actually made. The tool's definition of "at-risk." Its idea of what a workflow stage is. Its notion of which data fields are worth filtering on, which silently became your segmentation strategy.

That's not renewing a tool. That's renewing an org chart you have never been allowed to edit.

## You Don't Own Your Tools. You Rent Their Opinions.

Most leaders believe they own their platform. The truth is they own a login and a renewal obligation. The platform owns the shape of the team.

Here is the test that exposes it.

List your last five operational decisions about how your team actually works. How you define success for an account. How you prioritize your portfolio. What triggers an intervention. What a new team member is expected to do in their first week. How you measure performance.

Now check each one against a single question.

Could you change it tomorrow without filing a vendor ticket, waiting on a Professional Services scope, or being told "that's not how the platform handles it"?

Every decision that fails that test isn't yours. It belongs to the vendor, and you're renting it back from them every year.

## The Vendor-Default Ratio

The share of your operating model that exists because the tool ships that way, rather than because you deliberately chose it, is what I call your Vendor-Default Ratio.

You cannot read it off a dashboard. There's no report that surfaces it. But you can feel it the moment someone forces you to look.

High ratio means most of how your team works was inherited from a piece of software. Low ratio means you made deliberate choices and configured the tool to serve them, not the other way around.

Most organizations never measure it. And because they never measure it, it only ever drifts in one direction. Every quarter, the easiest path becomes more deeply grooved. Every new hire learns the tool's way as if it were the law of nature.

## How to Run the Audit

Before your next renewal, do three things.

First, write down your real operating decisions. Not the ones in the strategy deck. The ones that govern what happens day to day.

Second, trace each one back to its origin. Did a human decide this on purpose, or did the tool make it the default and nobody ever questioned it?

Third, separate the two piles. The deliberate decisions are yours. The defaults are the vendor's, and they make up your Vendor-Default Ratio.

My guess is the second pile will be larger than you'd be comfortable putting in writing.

That discomfort is the point. It's the number you've been managing a team by without ever being allowed to see it.

The goal isn't to rip out your platform. Switching tools just trades one set of inherited constraints for another. The goal is to know which constraints you actually chose, so that when you renew, you're choosing them on purpose instead of inheriting them by accident.

Most leaders will renew this quarter without running this audit. They'll take the discount and the clean procurement cycle. And they'll re-sign for another year inside definitions they never wrote and workflows their teams follow only because of the order of the screens.

Be the one who looks first.
