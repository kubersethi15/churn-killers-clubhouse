## Reddit (r/CustomerSuccess)

**Title:** How many of your CS team's operating decisions actually belong to your platform vendor?

Been thinking about this as a few renewals come up.

Most of us treat our CS platform as a system of record. But every one of these tools ships with an opinion baked in about how a CS motion should run. The default definition of at-risk. The way lifecycle stages are structured. Which fields are easy to filter on, which over time quietly becomes your segmentation strategy.

None of those started as deliberate decisions. They were defaults. And because the tool becomes invisible after a while, nobody ever re-examines them. You stop seeing the tool the same way you stop noticing the floor you're standing on.

The test I've found useful: list your last five operational decisions about how the team works. How you define a healthy account. How you tier the book. What triggers an intervention. What a CSM does in week one. How you measure success.

Then ask for each one: could you change it tomorrow without a vendor ticket, a Professional Services scope, or being told "that's not how the platform handles it"?

Every one that fails isn't really your decision. It's the vendor's, and you're renting it back every year.

Curious how others here think about this. Do you actively audit how much of your workflow is your choice vs the tool's default? Or does it just become invisible?

Kuber's newsletter went deep on this if anyone wants the full breakdown, but mostly curious about real experiences here.
