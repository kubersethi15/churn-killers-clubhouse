I call it the Vendor-Default Ratio.

It's the share of your operating model that exists because the tool ships that way, not because you chose it.

You can't read it off a dashboard. But you can feel it the moment someone makes you look.

Think about it.

The vendor's definition of "at-risk" became your definition.

The vendor's idea of a lifecycle stage became your lifecycle.

The fields the tool made easy to filter on silently became your segmentation strategy.

The order of the screens became your CSM workflow.

None of these were decisions. They were defaults. And defaults are decisions someone else made for you, then handed you the bill annually.

The scary part isn't that you inherited them. It's that they became invisible. The tool stopped being an object in the room and became the room itself.

You don't audit the floor you're standing on.

So here's my question for the CS leaders here.

Of the five biggest operational decisions in your function, how many could you actually change tomorrow without a vendor ticket?

Be honest in the comments. My guess is fewer than you'd want to put in writing.

For each one, ask: could I change this tomorrow without a vendor ticket or a Professional Services scope?

3. What triggers an intervention.
If "at-risk" means what the vendor decided at-risk means, your playbook isn't yours.

4. What a CSM does in week one.
If the onboarding follows the order of the screens, the UI designed your motion.

5. How you measure success.
If your reporting is whatever the dashboard exports cleanly, the tool picked your metrics.
