## Reddit (r/CustomerSuccess)

**Title:** Do customer health scores actually make CS teams worse at preventing churn?

I've been tracking this pattern across multiple CS teams: the accounts with the highest health scores are often the ones that churn without warning, while the "risky" low-scoring accounts end up renewing and expanding.

Started wondering if health scores are creating more problems than they solve. When your enterprise account shows 98% healthy across all metrics, but your CSM has a gut feeling something's off based on subtle changes in stakeholder behavior - which do you trust?

I've seen teams waste massive resources on false positive interventions (trying to "save" accounts that were never at risk) while missing the real risks because the algorithm said everything was fine.

The best CSMs I know have developed an intuitive sense of account health that goes way beyond usage metrics. They pick up on context that doesn't live in the CRM - budget conversations, internal politics, champion transitions.

Maybe we've over-indexed on trying to make customer relationships algorithmic when they're fundamentally human?

Wrote up my full thoughts on why health scores might be the astrology of customer success here: https://churnisdead.com/newsletter/case-against-customer-health-scores
