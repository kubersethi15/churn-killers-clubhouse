# Title: Why Your Net Revenue Retention Is Falling (And Why It Isn't Your Fault)
# Subtitle: Most SaaS leaders are confusing an expansion problem with a retention problem. The difference decides who gets blamed.

Across private SaaS, a quiet shift happened over the last three years. The median net revenue retention rate drifted from around 105% in 2021 to roughly 101% in 2024.

Four points does not sound like much. But the detail underneath it changes everything about who is responsible.

Most of that slide happened while gross retention barely moved.

That distinction matters more than almost anyone is treating it. So let me slow down and explain why.

## Two very different failures

Gross retention measures whether customers are leaving. Net retention measures whether they are growing. They sound similar. They are not.

If gross retention holds flat, your customers are sticking around. They are not churning. Whatever the team responsible for keeping customers is supposed to do, it is working.

If net retention falls anyway, it means those same customers stopped buying more. They renewed. They just did not expand.

Those are two completely different failures. The first is a retention failure. The second is an expansion failure. And in most companies, they get owned by the same person and rolled into the same number.

## The expansion recession nobody is naming

What we are living through is an expansion recession dressed up as a retention conversation.

Upsell has stalled. Cross-sell has stalled. Price increases have stalled. Budgets tightened across the board, and a new factor arrived on top of it: AI. Decision makers are now openly asking whether they even need to renew at the same level, because they suspect they can do more with less.

None of that is a value problem. Customers are getting value. They are staying. It is a pricing-power and product-roadmap problem. The company is not giving customers compelling new reasons to spend more, and the macro environment is making them defensive about every dollar.

This is the part that quietly traps Customer Success leaders. They proudly put net revenue retention on their goals slide. It was the number that finally earned the function a seat in the board deck, the proof that Customer Success drives revenue and not just goodwill.

Now that same number is falling for reasons no quarterly business review can fix. And the person who volunteered to own it is about to be asked why.

## One number doing three jobs

Here is what a falling retention number actually looks like inside a real book of business.

**The healthy flat account.** This customer adopted everything you sold them. Usage is strong. The champion is engaged. The renewal is locked. They love the product. They are also done. There is no new module, no new use case, no new tier to give them a reason to spend more this year. You did your job perfectly, and the account is flat.

**The price-clause account.** This account grew, but only because a contractual price escalator finally got enforced after years of being waived when inflation was near zero. The book grew. Nobody ran an expansion play. The clause did the work.

**The genuine expansion account.** A thin slice where something real landed. A second team adopted the product for a use case nobody scoped at signing. Real net-new value, real net-new spend.

All three of those roll into one blended retention number.

Leadership reads that single number as one clean signal of how well the team is driving growth. When it rises, the team gets credit for everything, including the price clause they never negotiated. When it falls, they inherit the entire drop, including the expansion the roadmap never gave them anything to sell.

That is the trap. One number is doing three jobs, and the team only controls one of them.

## The fix: bring three numbers, not one

The answer is to decompose the number before anyone decides what it means for you.

When the planning conversation comes, do not walk in defending a blended figure that drifted from 104% to 101%. Walk in with the breakdown.

**The Floor.** This is gross retention. It answers the only retention question that matters: are customers staying? If yes, this is healthy, and it is genuinely yours. Lead with it.

**The Clause.** This is growth that came from contractual price escalators rather than any expansion motion. Strip it out and label it. It inflates good years and conceals bad ones, and pretending it is expansion work fools no one for long.

**The Lever.** This is real net-new expansion. New use cases, new teams, new tiers. This is the only slice that reflects expansion motion you actually drove.

**The Lever Gap.** This is the distance between the expansion you ran and the expansion the roadmap made possible. Quantify it. This is the part most leaders never put on a slide, and it is the most important number in the room.

In one planning meeting I sat in, the Floor was flat and healthy, and the Lever Gap was larger than the entire net-expansion line. The room went quiet. Then the CFO asked the only question that mattered: "So how much of this is actually a pricing decision we haven't made?"

That is the question you want asked in your meeting. It only gets asked if you bring the breakdown that forces it.

Bring the blended number, and you will spend the next year explaining a decline you could not have prevented. Bring the decomposition, and you turn an interrogation into a strategy conversation about the ceiling, which is the only conversation that actually raises it.

The expansion recession is real, and it is not ending soon. The leaders who survive it will not be the ones who expanded hardest. They will be the ones who knew exactly which slice of the number was theirs, and said so out loud before anyone could decide it for them.
