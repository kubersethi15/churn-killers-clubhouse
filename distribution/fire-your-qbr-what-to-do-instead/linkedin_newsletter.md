# Title: Why QBRs Are Killing Your Customer Relationships

847 QBR invites sent last quarter. 312 customers declined immediately. 298 more rescheduled at least once. Of the 237 that actually happened, customers came prepared to exactly 31 of them.

This data comes from the head of enterprise success at a $200M ARR company. The rest were performance theater. CSMs presenting usage data customers already had. Customers nodding politely while checking Slack. Everyone pretending this was valuable relationship building.

Meanwhile, the highest-retention SaaS companies operate without QBRs entirely.

**The QBR Industrial Complex**

Here's what CS leaders tell themselves: "Our QBRs are strategic. We're business partners. We provide insights customers can't get elsewhere."

Here's what actually happens: You spend three days preparing slides about data your customer already monitors in real-time. You schedule a meeting they don't want to attend to tell them things they already know about their own business.

The QBR industrial complex has convinced us that customer success requires quarterly ceremony. That strategic relationships need scheduled check-ins. That business value must be delivered through conference rooms and screen shares.

This is backwards.

Customer success happens when customers achieve outcomes without thinking about your product. When insights arrive exactly when they need them. When expansion opportunities surface automatically based on usage patterns, not calendar reminders.

**How Winners Operate Instead**

Datadog doesn't do QBRs with their enterprise customers. Neither does Snowflake. Or MongoDB. These companies achieve 120%+ net revenue retention without quarterly PowerPoint presentations.

They built customer success into the product experience itself. Usage drives expansion automatically. Health monitoring happens in real-time. Strategic insights arrive as notifications, not meeting invites.

When Datadog customers see their infrastructure scaling, they get intelligent recommendations for additional monitoring capabilities. No CSM required. No meeting scheduled. The success experience is continuous and contextual.

**The Real Cost of QBR Theater**

Every QBR invitation is a withdrawal from your relationship bank account. When you ask customers to block time to hear you present their own data back to them, you're signaling that you don't understand their business.

When you schedule quarterly check-ins to discuss insights they could get on-demand, you're treating them like they need babysitting. When you create PowerPoint presentations about their usage patterns, you're admitting your product doesn't surface intelligence automatically.

Customers notice this. They start seeing you as overhead instead of outcome.
