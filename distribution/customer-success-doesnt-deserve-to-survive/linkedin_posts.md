Three lies CS teams tell themselves:

Lie 1: "We own the relationship."
You don't. The product does. If the product disappeared and you called the customer to chat, they wouldn't pick up.

Lie 2: "We prevent churn."
Most churn prevention is really churn detection after it's too late. The customer decided to leave six months ago. You found out two weeks before renewal. That's not prevention. That's a funeral arrangement.

Lie 3: "We need a seat at the table."
Engineering doesn't ask for a seat. Sales doesn't ask. Finance doesn't ask. They sit there because removing them would break something.

If removing CS wouldn't break anything, that's not a political problem. That's an impact problem.

Which of these hits closest to home for you?

POST 3 — THURSDAY
Strategy: Framework listicle, most shareable format
============================================================

Every CS team that survived the 2025 layoffs shares these 5 traits:

1. Revenue Attribution, not Revenue Adjacency
They can say "this customer renewed because I did X." Not "we were involved."

2. Technical Credibility, not Relationship Warmth
They add value in architecture reviews. Not just schedule them.

3. Decision Shaping, not Relationship Managing
Customers make different decisions because of their input. Not just different feelings.

4. Veto Power, not Just Responsibility
They flag bad-fit deals before close. Not clean up after.

5. Proof of Absence, not Presence
If they vanished, an operational process would break. Not just an emotional gap.

Quick test for your top 10 accounts:

"If I disappeared tomorrow, what specific process would break for this customer?"

If nothing breaks, you're replaceable. Time to build something that would.

POST 4 — FRIDAY

I ran the "proof of absence" test on my own accounts last month.

For each of my top 10 customers, I asked: "If I vanished tomorrow, what specific operational process would break?"

The results were uncomfortable.

For 3 accounts, the answer was clear. They relied on frameworks I built. Processes I designed. Reviews I ran quarterly. Removing me would create a real gap.

For 7 accounts, I was being honest with myself: they'd miss me, but nothing would actually break. They'd find workarounds. They'd be fine.

Seven out of ten.

That's the difference between being liked and being necessary. Between a relationship and an operational dependency.

I've been in CS for 10+ years. I know this stuff. And I still had a 30% hit rate on being truly embedded.
