Last month, Workday gutted 400 people from their Global Customer Operations team. Not sales. Not engineering. Customer operations.

Their official reason? "Realigning resources toward high-growth areas."

Translation: We're moving money away from humans who manage customers and toward AI and revenue-generating roles.

A week earlier, Salesforce quietly cut thousands, with customer support roles in the crosshairs.

The CS community responded exactly how you'd expect. LinkedIn erupted:

"CS is more important than ever!"
"Companies will regret this!"
"We just need to prove our value!"

Here's the part nobody wants to hear: **most CS teams don't deserve to survive.**

Before you close this tab, hear me out.

I'm not saying customer success as a concept is dead. Helping customers achieve outcomes is obviously critical. That's not going away.

What I am saying is that the way most CS teams operate has been a slow-moving credibility crisis for over a decade. And the layoffs aren't an injustice. They're a market correction.

**Here's what a typical CSM's week looks like:**

Monday: Standups about health scores nobody trusts.
Tuesday: QBR prep. Pulling screenshots the customer could pull themselves.
Wednesday: "Check-in" calls where the customer politely wonders why they're there.
Thursday: Chasing product for a feature update that won't ship this quarter.
Friday: Logging notes into a CRM nobody reads.

Which part of that is irreplaceable? Which part justifies a six-figure salary?

**The Three Lies CS Tells Itself**

**Lie #1: "We own the relationship."**
No, you don't. The product owns the relationship. If your product disappeared tomorrow and you called the customer to chat, they wouldn't pick up.

**Lie #2: "We prevent churn."**
Show me. Most "churn prevention" is really churn detection after it's too late. The customer decided to leave six months ago. You found out two weeks before renewal. That's not prevention. That's a funeral arrangement.

**Lie #3: "We need a seat at the table."**
Engineering doesn't ask for a seat. Sales doesn't ask. They sit there because removing them would break something. If removing CS wouldn't break anything, that's an impact problem.

**So what does survival look like?**

I spent the last year studying the teams that are untouchable. They share 5 characteristics I call the CS Survival Framework:

1. Revenue Attribution, Not Revenue Adjacency
2. Technical Credibility, Not Just Relationship Warmth
3. Customer Decision Shaping, Not Relationship Managing
4. Veto Power, Not Just Responsibility
5. Proof of Absence, Not Presence
