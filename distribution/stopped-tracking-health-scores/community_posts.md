# Community Cross-Posts -- I Stopped Tracking Health Scores. Here's What I Found Instead.

## Slack (Gain Grow Retain / CS Insider)

Just had a realization about health scores that's been bugging me. We spend so much time trying to predict which accounts will churn, but maybe we're solving the wrong problem entirely. 

I analyzed 6 months of churned accounts and found that only 23% were actually preventable by better CS execution. The rest? Budget cuts, executive changes, strategic pivots - stuff that no algorithm can predict.

What if we stopped trying to be fortune tellers and started being better at autopsies? Understanding what we could have controlled vs. what was always outside our influence?

I wrote more about this approach here: https://churnisdead.com/newsletter/stopped-tracking-health-scores

## Reddit (r/CustomerSuccess)

**Title:** Are we wasting time on health score predictions when we should focus on churn prevention?

Had a wake-up call recently when our "healthiest" enterprise account (95/100 health score) churned with 48 hours notice. New CFO, vendor consolidation, $2M contract gone.

Got me thinking - are we solving the wrong problem? Everyone's building better prediction algorithms, but maybe we need better prevention systems.

Did a deep dive on 6 months of churned accounts. Turns out only about 25% was actually preventable through better CS execution. The rest was budget cuts, strategic pivots, acquisitions - stuff no health score can predict.

Now instead of asking "which accounts will churn?" we ask "which churn could we have prevented?" Completely different resource allocation. Way more actionable.

Anyone else feeling like prediction theater is taking over actual prevention work? Been working on a framework to separate controllable vs uncontrollable churn that's been eye-opening.
