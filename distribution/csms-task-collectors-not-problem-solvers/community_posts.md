# Community Cross-Posts — Your CSMs Are Task Collectors, Not Problem Solvers

## Slack (Gain Grow Retain / CS Insider)

Had a realization this week about CSM effectiveness. Met someone who handled 247 customer requests this quarter with 98% response time. Sounds impressive, right?

Except when asked what problems she actually solved, she couldn't name three. She'd become a professional task collector. Create ticket, tag team, follow up, repeat.

The issue isn't capability. It's how we measure success. We reward response times over resolution quality. Motion over progress. Being busy over being valuable.

Your CSMs are closest to customer problems, which makes them best positioned to solve them. But first, you need to teach them the difference between managing requests and eliminating root causes.

## Reddit (r/CustomerSuccess)

**Title:** Are we training CSMs to be ticket routers instead of problem solvers?

Been thinking about CS team effectiveness lately. Noticed a pattern where our most 'organized' CSMs handle tons of requests but don't actually solve underlying problems.

They create tickets, tag the right teams, follow up religiously. Customers love their responsiveness. Leadership praises their organization. But the same issues keep recurring.

I think we've accidentally trained CSMs to be professional task collectors. We measure tickets closed, not problems eliminated. We celebrate response times, not resolution quality.

The thing is, CSMs are closest to customer problems, which should make them best positioned to solve them. But we've designed the role to reward delegation over investigation.

A task collector asks 'Who should handle this?' A problem solver asks 'Why is this happening?'

Seen this pattern at other companies? How do you balance speed of response with depth of resolution?

Wrote more thoughts on this shift from task collection to problem-solving in CS: https://churnisdead.com/newsletter/csms-task-collectors-not-problem-solvers
