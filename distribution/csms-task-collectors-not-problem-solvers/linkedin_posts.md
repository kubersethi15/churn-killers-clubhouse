Your CSMs are glorified ticket routers.

Met a CSM last week who handled 247 customer requests this quarter. Response time? 98%. Customer satisfaction? High. Problems actually solved? She couldn't name three.

This is the dirty secret of Customer Success. We've trained an entire generation of CSMs to be professional task collectors. They create tickets, tag teams, and follow up. But they don't solve problems.

We reward the wrong behaviors. Tickets closed over problems solved. Response times over resolution quality. Motion over progress.

The result? Customers keep having the same issues. CSMs feel busy but not valuable. And real problem-solving gets outsourced to 'experts' who don't understand customer context.

Your CSMs are closest to customer problems. Which means they're best positioned to solve them. But first, you need to stop measuring them like helpdesk agents.

============================================================

The best CSMs ask 'Why is this happening?' before 'Who should handle this?'

Most customer requests are symptoms, not root causes. Your customer says the API is timing out. A task collector creates a ticket for engineering. A problem solver asks deeper questions.

Is this happening during peak usage? Are they hitting rate limits? Is their implementation following best practices? Are they trying to solve the wrong business problem entirely?

The difference is methodology, not seniority. Problem-solving CSMs dig into context before delegating. They understand the customer's business goals, not just their immediate request.

This separates great CSMs from busy ones. Task collectors handle requests. Problem solvers eliminate the need for future requests.

Your customers don't need another person to route their tickets. They need someone who understands their world well enough to solve problems at the source.

What's the difference between your best and average CSMs? Is it technical knowledge or problem-solving approach?

Five signs your CSMs are task collectors, not problem solvers:

1. They measure success by tickets closed, not problems eliminated

2. They can tell you response times but not resolution patterns

3. The same customer issues keep recurring quarter after quarter

4. They escalate before investigating ('This is really an engineering issue')

5. They know your product features but not your customers' business outcomes

Task collection feels productive. Lots of activity. Busy Slack channels. High response rates.

But it's a trap. You're optimizing for speed over depth. Motion over progress. Customer satisfaction surveys over actual problem resolution.

Real problem solvers ask why before who. They dig into root causes before creating tickets. They understand customer context, not just product functionality.

The shift isn't about hiring different people. It's about rewarding different behaviors.

I used to think busy CSMs were good CSMs.

My first CS hire was incredibly organized. Detailed ticket tracking. Perfect follow-up cadence. Customers loved working with her because she was so responsive.

I promoted her. Gave her the biggest accounts. Praised her in team meetings.

Then I realized something uncomfortable. The same problems kept coming up. Different customers, same root issues. She was managing symptoms beautifully but never addressing causes.

It wasn't her fault. I had trained her to optimize for activity, not outcomes. Response time over resolution quality. Customer happiness over problem elimination.

The shift happened when I started asking different questions. Not 'How many tickets did you handle?' but 'What problems did you solve?' Not 'How fast did you respond?' but 'How did you prevent this from happening again?'

Changing the questions changed the behaviors. Task collectors became problem solvers.

Now I measure differently. And hire differently. And the results speak for themselves.
