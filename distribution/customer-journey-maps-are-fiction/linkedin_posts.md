The biggest predictor of customer success isn't where they are in your journey.

It's how they deviate from it.

I've analyzed hundreds of enterprise accounts. The pattern is clear:

Your highest-value customers broke your journey map early and often. They found shortcuts. They skipped stages. They created their own path to value.

Your churned accounts? They followed your mapped journey perfectly. Hit every milestone. Checked every box. Then quietly disappeared.

We design journey maps assuming customers want to follow our process. But successful customers don't want process. They want results.

They'll take whatever path gets them there fastest.

So instead of forcing customers into your journey, study the ones who ignore it. What shortcuts are they taking? What stages do they skip? What value do they find that you didn't map?

Then redesign your process around their deviations, not your assumptions.

The best journey maps aren't prescriptive. They're adaptive.

Three journey mapping lies every CS team believes:

1. All customers follow similar paths
Enterprise deals are snowflakes. Each implementation has unique technical requirements, different stakeholder dynamics, varying constraints. Using the same journey for a Fortune 500 manufacturer and Series B fintech is like using the same GPS route for a motorcycle and 18-wheeler.

2. Journey maps predict customer behavior
Maps show what should happen. They're terrible at predicting what will happen. Your biggest predictor isn't journey stage. It's deviation patterns. Successful customers break the map. Churned customers follow it perfectly.

3. Visual journey maps align teams
Beautiful maps create the illusion of alignment. Everyone nods in presentations. Everyone agrees on process. Then they return to desks and do exactly what they did before. Real alignment comes from shared metrics tied to actual customer outcomes, not theoretical touchpoints.

Stop building subway maps for demolition derby experiences.

Start designing adaptive frameworks that flex with customer reality.

I used to believe beautiful journey maps meant better customer outcomes.

I was wrong.

Early in my CS career, I spent weeks perfecting our journey map. Color-coded stages. Detailed touchpoints. Perfect handoff documentation. It was a work of art.

Our customer success metrics got worse.

Turns out, the more time we spent documenting the ideal journey, the less time we spent understanding actual customer behavior. We were solving theoretical problems while real customers struggled with issues we never mapped.

The breakthrough came when I stopped looking at our journey map and started looking at our most successful accounts. They all had one thing in common: they ignored our process entirely.

They found faster paths to value. They skipped stages we considered critical. They created workarounds for obstacles we didn't know existed.

Our job wasn't to force them back into our journey. It was to learn from their deviations and adapt our process accordingly.

Now I build adaptive frameworks, not rigid maps. Customers tell me what works. I document reality, not assumptions.

Better outcomes. Happier customers. Less beautiful slides.

Worth the trade.
