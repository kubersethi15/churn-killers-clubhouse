POST 1 — TUESDAY (hot take + newsletter link)
============================================================

AI isn't killing Customer Success.

It's exposing which parts of it were never defensible in the first place.

Every CS leader I talk to is being asked the same question: "How much of Customer Success can we automate now?"

On the surface, it sounds like an efficiency play. Underneath, it's something else entirely. It's a credibility audit.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: if an AI can do 70% of what a CSM does in a day, that doesn't mean AI is impressive. It means that 70% of the job was information delivery, status updates, and scheduling. Work that never required a human in the first place.

The CSMs who are safe aren't the ones fighting AI. They're the ones doing work AI can't touch: changing how customers think about their business, reshaping vendor decisions, building operational dependencies.

POST 2 — WEDNESDAY (pure value, no link, drives comments)
============================================================

A simple test to know if AI is coming for your CS role:

Look at your last 10 customer interactions. For each one, ask:

"Could an AI have done this with access to our CRM data and product usage?"

Status update email? Yes.
QBR slide deck with usage graphs? Yes.
Check-in call to ask how things are going? Probably yes.

Now ask:

"Could an AI have convinced the customer's CFO to renew a 3-year contract by reframing the ROI in terms their board would understand?"

No.

"Could an AI have told a customer their implementation approach was going to fail based on patterns you've seen across 40 similar deployments?"

No.

That's the line. Everything below it is exposed. Everything above it is your competitive advantage.

What percentage of your week falls above that line? Be honest.

POST 3 — THURSDAY (framework listicle)
============================================================

The 5% of CS work that AI will never replace:

1. Telling a customer they're wrong.
AI will surface data. It won't tell your VP of Engineering that their deployment strategy is going to fail. That takes trust, credibility, and the willingness to be uncomfortable.

2. Reshaping how a customer thinks about a decision.
AI can recommend. Humans reframe. "You're not choosing between two vendors. You're choosing between $300K in savings and $1.8M in operational risk."

3. Building political alignment inside a customer's org.
Renewals don't die because of product issues. They die because the champion left and nobody built a second relationship. AI doesn't navigate politics.

4. Saying no to a bad-fit deal before it closes.
AI optimizes. It doesn't have the conviction to tell sales "this customer will churn in 6 months and here's why."

5. Making a customer's team better at their jobs.
Not through a help article. Through sitting in their workflow, understanding their constraints, and recommending changes that stick.

If your week is full of these five things, AI is your assistant. If it isn't, AI is your replacement.

POST 4 — FRIDAY (personal story)
============================================================

I did something uncomfortable last month.

I mapped out every task I did in a typical week and labeled each one: "AI could do this" or "AI can't do this."

The results were not flattering.

Roughly 60% of my week was information delivery. Pulling data for QBRs. Writing status updates. Summarizing support tickets. Prepping internal reports. All things an AI with access to our systems could handle.

The other 40% was the real work. Convincing a customer to restructure their implementation. Pushing back on an internal deal that was going to churn in 90 days. Running a business impact analysis that saved a renewal.

60/40. That was my split. And I've been doing this for over a decade.

The question isn't whether AI is coming for CS. It already has. The question is whether you're spending enough of your week on the 40% that's actually yours.
