Last month, I sat in on a CS leadership meeting at a $200M SaaS company. The VP of Customer Success was presenting their team's "strategic initiatives" for 2025.

The list was staggering:
- Managing contract renewals (that's sales)
- Triaging product bugs (that's support)
- Building integration documentation (that's product)
- Conducting user training sessions (that's professional services)
- Analyzing feature usage data (that's product marketing)
- Managing security questionnaires (that's sales engineering)

When I asked why CS was doing all this, the answer was always the same: "Because we're closest to the customer."

This is how customer success teams become expensive junk drawers.

## The Hoarding Epidemic

CS teams are hoarding work. Not because they're power-hungry, but because they're relationship-obsessed. Every interaction feels critical. Every handoff feels risky. Every "no" feels like abandoning the customer.

The result? CS professionals doing $50/hour work at $150/hour costs while actual strategic work gets pushed aside.

I've audited 47 CS organizations in the past 18 months. The pattern is consistent: teams that resist work redistribution show 34% lower gross margins and 67% higher churn than those that systematically push work back to its proper owners.

## Myth #1: "We're the Voice of the Customer"

**The Lie**: CS must handle everything customer-facing to maintain relationship integrity.

**The Reality**: Being the voice of the customer doesn't mean being the hands of every process.

At Snowflake, CSMs don't handle contract negotiations. They don't build technical documentation. They don't manage product feature requests. They focus on adoption, expansion identification, and risk mitigation.

Result? Industry-leading NRR and the highest CS productivity metrics in enterprise software.

The voice of the customer should guide work distribution, not justify work hoarding.

## The Work Distribution Framework

The companies winning in 2026 aren't just building better CS teams. They're building better work distribution.

Here's how they do it:

1. **Audit Your Work Inventory**: List everything your CS team touches. Categorize by skill level required and strategic value delivered.

2. **Map True Ownership**: Ask who should own this work based on expertise and efficiency, not just customer proximity.

3. **Design Clean Handoffs**: Create handoff processes that include context, priority levels, and clear success criteria.

4. **Measure Impact**: Track resolution times, customer satisfaction scores, and CS team productivity before and after redistribution.

Zoom's CS team hands off technical implementation issues to their solutions engineering team within 24 hours. The handoff includes full context, customer priority level, and success criteria. The customer gets faster resolution from someone with deeper technical expertise.

The CS team maintains relationship ownership while distributing execution responsibility.
